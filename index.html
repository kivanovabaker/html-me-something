Confessions of an Immigrant or Ode to Multiculturalism

People have always had a lot to say about immigrants. Whether hated, feared, pitied, or revered, there has never been a lack of emotion in regard to strange new folks that don't quite fit. Our ancestors relied on close social networks to stay alive, so perhaps it is natural to fear those who are not like us. However, this same fear hinders progress, engenders hate, leads to senseless bloodshed, and prevents immigrants from being seen first and foremost as simply people. The following paragraphs are just one short glimpse at a growing human that flew 5,508 miles and worked to make a new home in St. Louis, MO.

Roots

Hello, my name is Kseniya. I like to say that I grew up in Kharkov, Ukraine. In reality, I was just a few months short of 11 when we arrived in the February of 2002. Where that fits on the spectrum of growing up, I'm still not sure. While I was still malleable, my core values and beliefs were already formed, of rich black dirt, wheat, and potatoes - I like to think.

I had grown up a happy child, born on the first of May or International Workers' Day for much of the world, very conveniently always a non-working holiday throughout the former Soviet Republics. I was in love with life, had a best friend that doubled as a siamese twin, was class president (yes, we start having those in 4th/5th grade. You also keep your class of about 30 people throughout most of your school career), and was consistently in love with someone/anyone/everyone (who knows). I was an optimistic child.
